The buzz around climate change involves the many features which defines it as the goliath of a problem it is. It's a multi-layered, slow-acting problem which involves the entire world, and therefore requires global collaboration. Any sincere effort to slow climate change down in one part of the world can quite easily be offset by negligence elsewhere. How can we solve this? Where do we look?

Renewable energy dominates the narrative when we think about progress. Fossil fuel consumption, one of the primary drivers of our deteriorating climate is challenged by the renewable alternatives which are becoming increasingly present in the modern world. Electric cars, ethanol and fuel efficient and solar panels are a few of the things marketed tol rescue us from this ever-looming doom, and rightly so. In recognising this, some countries distinguish themselves from the rest of the world - Brazil and Norway are two in particular who dominate the race for renewable.

Brazil is one of few countries with almost half of its primary energy coming from renewable energy. Being the world's largest producer of ethanol, Brazil stands as a leader in biofiel and waste energy production. Additionally, more than 70% of cars in Brazil running on ethanol. These qualities distinguish Brazil when it comes to renewable energy, and it is easy to see why the country can be considered a standard for the rest of the world.
Norway has the highest share of renewable energy in the world. What makes Norway unique is that it utilises hydropower more than any other country around the globe. In 2018, 95% of Norways electricity was generated purely through hydropower. [reference]
